A study on $CO_2$ emissions produced by automotive vehicles¶

09/03/2021 - Fabrizio Boffa¶

1. Data¶

For this study our source of data is the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).
EPA's National Vehicle and Fuel Emissions Laboratory tests new cars and trucks sold in the U.S. to ensure that they comply with federal emissions and fuel economy standards. The dataset is updated weekly and we will use the last update available at the date.
At the end of this study we will dedicate a paragraph about the quality of the used data while now we will proceed straight to the data acquisition process.

We will import these categorical features:

  • VClass = EPA vehicle size class
  • make = manufacturer (division)
  • drive = drive axle type
  • fuelType1 = primary fuel type. For single fuel vehicles, this will be the only fuel. For dual fuel vehicles, this will be the conventional fuel.
  • fuelType2 = secondary fuel type. For dual fuel vehicles, this will be the alternative fuel (e.g. E85, Electricity, CNG, LPG).
  • tCharger = if T, the vehicle is turbocharged
  • sCharger = if S, the vehicle is supercharged

and these numerical features:

  • year = model year
  • displ = engine displacement in liters
  • cylinders = engine cylinders
  • co2TailpipeGpm = tailpipe $CO_2$ emissions in grams/mile testing with fuelType1
  • co2TailpipeAGpm = tailpipe $CO_2$ emissions in grams/mile testing with fuelType2 (if hybrid)

To define the vehicle VClass, EPA uses this segmentation:

  • Two-Seaters = Any (cars designed to seat only two adults)
  • Minicompact Cars = Less than 85 cu ft.
  • Subcompact Cars = 85 to 99 cu ft.
  • Compact Cars = 100 to 109 cu ft.
  • Mid-Size Cars = 110 to 119 cu ft.
  • Large Cars = 120 or more cu ft.
  • Small Station Wagons = Less than 130 cu ft.
  • Mid-Size Cars Station Wagons = 130 to 159 cu ft.
  • Large Cars Station Wagons = 160 or more cu ft.
  • Small Pickup Trucks = Less than 4,500 lbs GVWR through Model Year 2007
  • Small Pickup Trucks = Less than 6,000 lbs GVWR after Model Year 2008
  • Standard Pickup Trucks = 4,500 to 8,500 lbs GVWR through Model Year 2007
  • Standard Pickup Trucks = 6,000 to 8,500 lbs GVWR after Model Year 2008
  • Passenger Vans = Less than 8,500 lbs GVWR through Model Year 2010
  • Passenger Vans = Less than 10,000 lbs GVWR beginning Model Year 2011
  • Cargo Vans = Less than 8,500 lbs GVWR
  • Minivans = Less than 8,500 lbs GVWR
  • Sport Utility Vehicles (SUVs) = Less than 8,500 lbs GVWR through Model Year 2010
  • Sport Utility Vehicles (SUVs) = Less than 10,000 lbs GVWR beginning Model Year 2011
  • Small Sport Utility Vehicles (SUVs) = Less than 6,000 lbs GVWR beginning Model Year 2013
  • Standard Sport Utility Vehicles (SUVs) = 6,000 to 10,000 lbs GVWR beginning Model Year 2013
  • Special Purpose Vehicles = Less than 8,500 lbs GVWR through Model Year 2010
  • Special Purpose Vehicles = Less than 10,000 lbs GVWR beginning Model Year 2011

Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR) is calculated as truck weight plus carrying capacity.

The goal of this study is to find answers to these questions:

  • Does Premium gasoline produce less $CO_2$ emissions than Regular?
  • Are vehicles designed by USA manufacturers more polluting than the others?
  • Are newer designed vehicles less polluting than older ones?

Since the EPA dataset is not representative of the population of the vehicles in service, it's important to note that we can respond to these questions only from a technological perspective and not from a real environmental impact perspective. In other words, we could discover that newer car models are technically more ecological than older ones, but since we don't know how many new models are on the streets respect older ones we can't desume their actual impact on the environment.

For model year 2013 and beyond, tailpipe $CO_2$ emssions are based on EPA tests. For previous model years, $CO_2$ is estimated using an EPA emission factor.
Given this, we will focus only on data relative to vehicles models from 2013 included till today.

3.2 Outliers¶

In this scenario we think that retaining outlier values during our tests is the best choice.

This conclusion comes from different considerations:

  1. the source of the data is very reliable as we will see in a later paragraph of this study. This means that it's very unlikely the presence of erroneous values.
  2. we will keep into account the possible outliers presence by choosing a methodology that is resistant to their effect.

As a partial confirmation of the first statement, we have checked data for the presence of really far out values. We've used the famous Tukey's fences method, based on the interquartile range:

$[Q_1 - k(Q_3 - Q_1), Q_3 + k(Q_3 - Q_1)]$

Using the values of $k$ proposed by John Tukey, the rule states that values outside that range are:

  • "outlier" values, for $k = 1.5$
  • "far out" values, for $k = 3$

That said, below we find the only value classifiable as a far out value.

4. Key Findings and Insights¶

4.1 Premium and Regular gasoline $CO_2$ emissions analysis¶

Let's focus on Premium and Regular gasoline engines by watching the histogram of $CO_2$ emissions below.

At first glance, watching the medians values showed by the dashed lines, we might hastily conclude that Premium gasoline is more polluting than Regular.

Is it possible that maybe those vehicles designed to run on Premium gasoline are also designed to be more performant and polluting than those meant for Regular gasoline?

In the graphs below, we clearly see that engines designed to run on Premium are also generally bigger in displacement and number of cylinders. We can see median displacement values of 2.5 and 3.0 litres and median numbers of cylinders of 4 and 6 as shown by the dashed lines.
We can also see that Premium based engines tend to be supercharged and turbocharged more often than engines meant for Regular gasoline.

For our study case this is a concern because it means that we could have a biased sample in relation to our analysis goals.

Indeed we can easily find different studies on the web stating that:

  • the larger the engine displacement, the higher the average fuel consumption and $CO_2$ emission
  • the emission levels of $NO_x$, $CO_2$, and $CO_2$ are higher in terms of their concentration in the exhaust fume of the turbocharged engine, in comparison with the naturally aspirated engine
  • there is a linear relationship between average fuel consumption and $CO_2$ emission of an automobile and its mass

A positive correlation between the aforementioned engines characteristics and $CO_2$ emissions is also clearly visible in the plots below for both Premium and Regular gasoline.

Now it's clear that we have a sampling bias in this scenario because the amount of $CO_2$ emissions is not only determined by the gasoline type that is on study, but also by confounders that impact differently on the two samples.
In simple words, it's rather obvious that Premium gasoline will result being more pollutant than regular Gasoline if our test is based on a Regular gasoline 2.5 litres aspirated compact car versus a Premium gasoline 3.0 litres turbocharged SUV!

To allow us to draw our conclusions we need to minimize the effect of the confounding variables and, we think, the best way to do it in this scenario is by "matching".
With matching, we select two groups so that each member of the first group should have a counterpart in the second group with the same values of potential confounders, but different independent variable values (in this case, Premium and Regular gasoline).

Without further ado, we will match the groups defined by the independent variable "fuelType1" on these clear and potential confounders:

  • vehicle class
  • drive system
  • engine displacement
  • engine cylinders
  • engine charger
  • manufacturer area
  • model age

To do so we set up an algorithm that:

  1. groups the dataset by all the confounders,
  2. drops all vehicles that are not in groups containing at least 3 units of either Premium and Regular gasoline,
  3. if a group has not an equal number of Premium and Regular vehicles, randomly removes those in excess until parity is achieved,
  4. finally create a test dataset with the remaining veichles.

We finally obtain a test dataset with an equal number of Premium and Regular gasoline models and an equal distribution of confounders between the two fuel types values.

5. Hypothesis formulation and significance test¶

Now is time to formulate some hypothesis based on the insights we've gathered so far.

Summarizing, we wanted to look evidence about differences $CO_2$ emissions based on:

  • type of primary fuel, either Premium or Regular gasoline,
  • manufacturer area, either US or not US,
  • models ages, either older, so from 2013 to 2017 included, or newer.

The insights we've drawn watching the previous distributions are that:

  • $CO_2$ emissions from Premium gasoline seem to be slightly less than those from Regular gasoline,
  • the models designed by US manufacturers seem to be more polluting than those designed by non US ones,
  • $CO_2$ emissions from new models seem to be slightly less than those from old models,

Let's now go through significance testing process case by case.

5.1 'Regular' and 'Premium' gasoline $CO_2$ emissions¶

We will test the null hypothesis $H_{0}$ that Regular gasoline is more or equally polluting than Premium gasoline. Consequently the alternative hypothesis $H_{A}$ will be that Premium gasolin is less polluting than Regular. So this will be a one sided test.

First of all we have to check the two distributions for normality and, looking at the previous histograms, it seems that this is not the case.
To confirm our foreseas, we will test both distributions with the Shapiro-Wilk test.

The Shapiro–Wilk test is a test of normality in frequentist statistics. It was published in 1965 by Samuel Sanford Shapiro and Martin Wilk. $H_{0}$ of this test is that the population is normally distributed. Thus, if the p-value is less than the chosen significance level $\alpha$, then $H_{0}$ is rejected and there is evidence that the data tested are not normally distributed. On the other hand, if the p-value is greater than the chosen $\alpha$ level, then $H_{0}$ can not be rejected, so there is statistical evidence that data came from a normally distributed population.

Below we find the test results with $\alpha$ = 0.05.

The Shapiro-Wilk test strongly confirms that we are far away from normal distributions and suggests us to rely on non parametric statistical tests.

Given this and considering that we've decided to retain possible outlier values, we choose to test significance with the Mann–Whitney U test.

The Mann–Whitney U test (also called the Mann–Whitney–Wilcoxon (MWW), Wilcoxon rank-sum test, or Wilcoxon–Mann–Whitney test) is a a ranks based and non parametric test to compare two independant samples on the $H_{0}$ that they are drawn from two populations stochastically equivalent. This means that for randomly selected values X and Y from both populations, the probability of X being greater than Y is equal to the probability of Y being greater than X. Consequently, $H_{A}$ is that one population is stochastically greater than the other, thus the probability of X being greater than Y is not equal to the probability of Y being greater than X.

The Mann–Whitney U test is commonly used instead of the two independent samples t-test when data are not normally distributed.

Being a ranks based test, it is robust to outliers presence. This is because, when the data are ranked, an outlier will simply be recognized as a case that is ranked one above or below the next less extreme case. So the effective value of the difference does not affect the test.

Below we find the outcome of the significance test with $\alpha$ = 0.05.

The Mann–Whitney U test confirms our previous insight, so we have a statistical evidence that gasoline type affects vehicles $CO_2$ emissions and Premium one is less polluting than Regular one.

6. Next steps in analyzing data¶

Looking at what we have found so far, we think there are two points we could study more deeply.

Relatively to the Premium and Regular gasoline, we have found evidences about a statistcal difference in $CO_2$ emissions from Premium and Regular gasoline.
Nevertheless the p-value we calculate with the Mann-Whitney U-test was about 0.013, thus not so far from the significance level chosen (0.05). Also, as we remember, the testing dataset was produced using random sampling. Precisely on a total of 1629 samples, 727 were randomly removed to balance the number of Premium and Regular samples in each group.

Since we've sacrificed almost one half of the information to mitigate the effect of confounders, we want to check if the outcome is still valid putting back somehow the missing information.

Relatively to "New" and "Old" models, we saw how the Mann - Whitney U-test has strongly confirmed statistical significance even if the histograms didn't show a sensible difference. Thus, it will be interesting to further investigate this outcome.

6.1 Verifying that Premium gasoline is less polluting than Regular gasoline¶

Here, we will check if the random sampling process used to assemble the testing dataframe has led us, just by chance, to fail to reject the $H_0$.

So we will again regroup the original dataset on the confounding variables with these same steps already implemented:

  1. groups the dataset by all the confounders,
  2. for each group, checks and removes outliers with the "Interquartile range" rule,
  3. drops all vehicles that are not in groups containing at least 3 units of either Premium and Regular gasoline.

Then we will perform $N$ iterations of these tasks:

  1. balances the number of Premium and Regular vehicles in each group by randomly removing those in excess,
  2. performs a Mann Mann - Whitney U-test on the resulting dataset,
  3. add the resultin p-value in a list.

Since we have multiple testing we need to account the fact that we could find small p-values < $\alpha$ just by pure chance.
One of the most commonly used approaches to control familywise error rate (FWER) in multiple comparisons is the Bonferroni correction, but in this case we consider it to be too stringent. Instead, since we can consider this as an exploratory study, we will proceed by controlling false discovery rate (FDR).
FDR controlling procedures provide less stringent control of Type I errors (false positives) compared to FWER controlling procedures (such as the Bonferroni correction), which control the probability of at least one Type I error. Thus, FDR-controlling procedures have greater power, at the cost of increased numbers of Type I errors.

So, after completing the $N$ iterations obtaining $N$ p-values, we will correct them using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure to control the FDR at a level $\alpha$ = 0.05.

7. Quality of the data¶

We have acquired data from https://www.fueleconomy.gov/ which is the official U.S. government source for fuel economy information. This website is administered by Oak Ridge National Laboratory for the U.S. Department of Energy and the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency.
About emissions testing, data is derived from vehicle testing done at National Vehicle and Fuel Emissions Laboratory and by vehicle manufacturers who submit their own test data to EPA for verification.

All new cars and trucks sold in the U.S. must be certified to meet federal emission standards, such as limits on the amount of smog-forming and greenhouse gas emissions that they can produce. Most testing is performed by auto manufacturers at their own facilities. EPA then audits the data and performs its own testing on some of the vehicles to confirm the manufacturers' results.
Below a description of the testing process:

  1. The vehicle is tested in a dynamometer test cell, which works like a treadmill for cars.
  2. A driver operates the vehicle following driving "schedules," which simulate typical trips (including specific speeds and acceleration, different temperatures, use of A/C, etc).
  3. A hose is connected to the tailpipe to collect engine exhaust. Carbon is measured to calculate the amount of fuel burned. Other air pollutants are measured using gas analyzers, and fine particles (particulate matter, PM) are weighed in a special "clean room" (some of these methods will differ for alternative-fueled vehicles, such as electric vehicles).

Other than vehicle emission certification, the same dataset is also used by:

  • the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) to publish the annual Fuel Economy Guide,
  • the U.S. Department of Transportation (DOT) to administer the Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) program,
  • the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) to collect "Gas Guzzler" taxes.

As a consequence of the above, the dataset it's very reliable and our choice to retain outliers is well justified.

Unfortunately the dataset does not contain information about the vehicles mass and the engine power that would have been ideal for a deeper comparison between $CO_2$ emissions from similar mass / power samples.
